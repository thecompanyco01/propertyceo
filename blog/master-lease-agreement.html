Master Lease Agreement: Complete Guide for Real Estate Investors

March 8, 2026 · 13 min read · By PropertyCEO

What if you could control a 50-unit apartment building, collect all the rent, and profit from it — without actually owning it? That's the power of a master lease agreement.

Master leases are one of the most underused strategies in real estate investing. They let you take operational control of a property, manage it for profit, and often include an option to purchase — all with significantly less capital than a traditional acquisition.

This guide covers everything you need to know: what a master lease is, how it works, the different types, the benefits and risks, and how savvy investors use master leases to build portfolios without massive down payments.

💡 Key takeaway: A master lease lets you control and profit from a property without owning it. You lease the entire property from the owner, sublease individual units to tenants, and keep the spread. Combined with a purchase option, it's one of the most creative acquisition strategies in real estate.

What Is a Master Lease Agreement?

A master lease agreement is a contract where one party — the master tenant (or master lessee) — leases an entire property from the owner (the landlord or lessor). The master tenant then has the right to sublease individual units, spaces, or portions of the property to end tenants.

Here's how the structure works:

  • Property owner → leases the entire property to the master tenant
  • Master tenant → pays agreed rent to the owner and subleases to end tenants
  • End tenants → pay rent to the master tenant

The master tenant's profit comes from the spread — the difference between what they pay the owner and what they collect from subtenants. If you master-lease a 10-unit building for $8,000/month and collect $12,000/month from tenants, you profit $4,000/month before expenses.

Master Lease vs. Traditional Lease

A traditional lease is between a landlord and a single tenant for one unit or space. A master lease covers the entire property and gives the master tenant operational control — including the right to sublease, set rental rates, manage tenants, and make improvements (within the terms of the agreement).

How Does a Master Lease Work?

Let's walk through a typical master lease scenario:

The Setup

Imagine an apartment building owner with a 20-unit property that's 60% occupied and poorly managed. The owner is tired of dealing with tenants, vacancies, and maintenance. They're barely breaking even and considering selling.

You approach the owner with a master lease proposal: you'll lease the entire building, pay a fixed monthly rent, and handle all property management. The owner gets guaranteed income without the headaches. You get operational control and the opportunity to turn the property around.

The Numbers

Metric Current (Owner) After Master Lease (You)
Total units 20 20
Occupied units 12 (60%) 18 (90%)
Avg. rent/unit $800 $950 (after improvements)
Gross rental income $9,600/mo $17,100/mo
Master lease payment — $10,000/mo to owner
Operating expenses $6,000/mo $4,500/mo (more efficient)
Net income $3,600/mo $2,600/mo (your profit)

The owner now makes $10,000/mo guaranteed (up from $3,600 net). You make $2,600/mo in profit — and you didn't buy the property. As you continue improving occupancy and rents, your profit grows while the owner's payment stays fixed.

Types of Master Leases

1. Standard Master Lease

A straightforward arrangement where you lease the property, pay fixed rent, and manage it for profit. No purchase option is included. This is common in corporate housing, student housing, and hospitality.

2. Master Lease with Option to Purchase (MLO)

This is the most popular version among investors. It combines the master lease with an option to buy the property at a predetermined price within a specified time frame (typically 3–10 years).

The MLO is powerful because:

  • You lock in a purchase price today while improving the property
  • As you increase NOI, the property's value goes up — but your purchase price stays the same
  • You can often use the improved property to secure financing for the purchase
  • It requires very little upfront capital compared to a traditional acquisition

3. Triple Net (NNN) Master Lease

In a triple net master lease, the master tenant is responsible for all property expenses: taxes, insurance, and maintenance — in addition to the base rent. This gives the owner completely passive income and the master tenant maximum control.

4. Sandwich Lease

A sandwich lease is a type of master lease where the master tenant sits "in between" the owner and the end tenants. The master tenant has obligations to the owner above and the subtenants below. This structure is common in commercial real estate and corporate leasing.

Benefits of Master Lease Agreements

For the Master Tenant (Investor)

  • Low capital requirements: Control a property with minimal upfront money — no down payment, no mortgage qualification
  • Immediate cash flow: Start collecting rent from day one without the cost of acquisition
  • Forced appreciation: Improve the property's NOI, which increases its value — value you can capture with a purchase option
  • Portfolio growth: Master lease multiple properties simultaneously, scaling your portfolio faster than traditional acquisitions
  • Risk mitigation: If the deal goes sideways, you walk away from a lease — you don't have a mortgage underwater
  • Property management experience: Build your property management track record without ownership risk

For the Property Owner

  • Guaranteed income: A fixed payment regardless of vacancies
  • No management headaches: The master tenant handles all day-to-day operations
  • Property preservation: An engaged master tenant maintains and improves the property
  • Continued ownership: They keep the asset (and its tax benefits) until/unless the purchase option is exercised
  • Potential sale: The purchase option provides a built-in exit strategy at a known price

Risks of Master Lease Agreements

Master leases aren't without risk. Here are the key dangers and how to mitigate them:

1. Vacancy Risk

You owe the owner rent every month regardless of occupancy. If you can't fill units, you're paying out of pocket. Mitigation: Negotiate a ramp-up period with reduced rent during the first 6–12 months while you stabilize occupancy.

2. Deferred Maintenance

If the property needs more work than expected, repair costs can eat into your margins. Mitigation: Conduct thorough inspections before signing. Negotiate a maintenance reserve or cap on repair expenses.

3. Owner Default

If the property owner stops paying their mortgage, the bank can foreclose — and your master lease may be terminated. Mitigation: Include lease provisions that survive foreclosure. Verify the owner's mortgage status and financial health. Consider a memorandum of lease recorded against the title.

4. Limited Control

You don't own the property, so you may have restrictions on major renovations, refinancing, or structural changes. Mitigation: Negotiate broad rights in the master lease agreement — especially regarding improvements, signage, and sublease terms.

5. Subtenant Problems

You're responsible for managing subtenants, including problem tenants. Eviction costs and processes fall on you. Mitigation: Use thorough tenant screening, maintain reserves for legal costs, and build proper notice procedures into your sublease agreements.

6. Legal Complexity

Master lease agreements are legally complex and must comply with local landlord-tenant laws, zoning regulations, and sometimes securities laws. Mitigation: Always work with a real estate attorney experienced in master lease structures.

Master Lease vs. Sublease: What's the Difference?

Feature Master Lease Sublease
Parties Owner → Master Tenant Master Tenant → End Tenant
Scope Entire property Individual unit or space
Control Operational control of the property Occupancy of a specific unit
Responsibility Management, maintenance, subleasing Rent payment, unit care
Contract with owner Yes — direct agreement No — contract with master tenant

The master lease is the "umbrella" agreement. Subleases are the individual contracts underneath it. The property owner only has a relationship with the master tenant — not with any subtenants.

Use Cases for Master Leases

Multifamily Value-Add

Master leasing underperforming apartment buildings is perhaps the most common use case. You take over a poorly managed property, improve occupancy and rents, and profit from the turnaround. If you have a purchase option, you can buy the property at its "before" price and own it at its "after" value.

Commercial Real Estate

Master leases are standard in commercial real estate — particularly for office buildings, retail centers, and mixed-use properties. Corporate tenants, co-working spaces, and restaurant groups often master-lease entire floors or buildings.

Student Housing

Student housing operators frequently master-lease properties near universities, then rent individual rooms to students at a premium. The per-bed revenue model creates significant spreads.

Short-Term Rental Arbitrage

The Airbnb arbitrage model is essentially a master lease strategy — you lease a property long-term and sublease it short-term on platforms like Airbnb and VRBO. When done right, the short-term income significantly exceeds the long-term lease payment.

Corporate Housing

Companies that provide furnished corporate housing often master-lease units in apartment buildings and rent them to traveling professionals at 2–3x the base rent.

Key Terms to Include in Your Master Lease Agreement

Every master lease should address these critical terms:

  1. Rent amount and escalations: Fixed rent with clear terms on any annual increases
  2. Lease term: Duration of the master lease (typically 5–10 years)
  3. Purchase option: Price, exercise period, and conditions for buying the property
  4. Subleasing rights: Explicit permission to sublease individual units
  5. Maintenance responsibilities: Who handles what — day-to-day vs. capital repairs
  6. Improvement rights: What renovations and modifications you can make
  7. Insurance requirements: Who carries what coverage
  8. Default and remedies: What happens if either party defaults
  9. Subordination: How the master lease interacts with the owner's mortgage
  10. Assignment: Whether you can assign the master lease to another party
  11. Ramp-up period: Reduced rent during the initial stabilization period
  12. Memorandum of lease: A recorded document that puts the world on notice of your lease interest

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a master lease agreement?

A master lease agreement is a contract where one party (the master tenant) leases an entire property from the owner, then has the right to sublease individual units or spaces to end tenants. The master tenant pays rent to the property owner and collects rent from subtenants, profiting from the spread. It's commonly used in commercial real estate, multifamily properties, and as a creative investing strategy.

How does a master lease with option to purchase work?

A master lease with option to purchase (MLO) combines a master lease with the right to buy the property at a predetermined price within a specified time frame. The master tenant controls and manages the property, improves its performance, and can exercise the purchase option later — often at the original agreed price even if the property has appreciated.

What is the difference between a master lease and a sublease?

A master lease is the primary lease between the property owner and the master tenant for the entire property. A sublease is a secondary lease between the master tenant and an end tenant for a portion of the property. The master tenant is both a tenant (to the owner) and a landlord (to the subtenants).

Is a master lease agreement legal?

Yes, master lease agreements are legal and widely used in commercial real estate. However, they must comply with local landlord-tenant laws, zoning regulations, and any existing lease restrictions. Always have a real estate attorney review your master lease agreement before signing.

What are the risks of a master lease agreement?

Key risks include: vacancy risk (you owe rent to the owner even if units are empty), maintenance costs eating into margins, difficulty evicting subtenants, the owner defaulting on their mortgage, limited control over major property decisions, and potential liability for property conditions.

Who pays for repairs in a master lease?

Repair responsibilities are defined in the master lease agreement and are fully negotiable. In many master leases, the master tenant handles day-to-day repairs and maintenance while the property owner remains responsible for major structural and capital repairs. Some master leases are "triple net" where the master tenant handles everything.

Final Thoughts

Master lease agreements are one of the most powerful — and most overlooked — tools in real estate investing. They let you control properties, generate cash flow, and build a portfolio with minimal capital. For aspiring property managers and investors who don't have hundreds of thousands for down payments, master leases can be the door that opens everything else.

But they're not without risk. The key to success is thorough due diligence, a well-drafted agreement reviewed by an experienced attorney, and the operational skill to actually improve the property. If you can do those three things, master leases can accelerate your real estate career dramatically.
