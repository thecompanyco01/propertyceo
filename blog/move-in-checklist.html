Move-In Checklist for Landlords & Property Managers [Free Template]

March 8, 2026 · 11 min read · By PropertyCEO

The move-in process is one of the most important moments in any tenancy. Get it right, and you set the foundation for a smooth, profitable relationship. Get it wrong, and you're looking at disputes, lost security deposits, and potentially expensive legal battles.

This guide gives you a comprehensive move-in checklist, a condition inspection template, and practical advice for making every tenant move-in professional, documented, and legally defensible.

💡 Why this matters: Over 40% of security deposit disputes stem from poor documentation at move-in. A thorough checklist is your best legal protection — and it takes less than an hour.

Why You Need a Move-In Checklist

A move-in checklist serves three critical purposes:

  1. Legal protection. It documents the property's condition when the tenant takes possession. If there's a dispute at move-out over damage vs. normal wear and tear, this is your evidence.
  2. Professionalism. A structured move-in process signals to tenants that you take property management seriously. It sets expectations for how the tenancy will be managed.
  3. Accountability. Both you and the tenant sign off on the property's condition. No one can claim the stain on the carpet or the crack in the countertop wasn't there when they moved in.

In many states, a move-in inspection is legally required before you can deduct from a security deposit at move-out. Even where it's not mandatory, it's your strongest defense in any deposit dispute. For more on security deposit laws, see our Complete Guide to Security Deposit Laws.

Before Move-In Day: Preparation Checklist

The move-in process starts well before the tenant arrives with a moving truck. Here's what to handle in advance:

🔧 Property Preparation (1-2 Weeks Before)

  • Deep clean the entire unit (or hire a professional cleaning service)
  • Touch up paint — walls, baseboards, trim, doors
  • Repair any outstanding maintenance items
  • Replace HVAC filters
  • Test all smoke detectors and carbon monoxide detectors (replace batteries)
  • Check all door locks and deadbolts — rekey if previous tenant didn't return all keys
  • Test all appliances: stove/oven, refrigerator, dishwasher, microwave, garbage disposal, washer/dryer
  • Run all faucets and check for leaks (kitchen, bathrooms, utility areas)
  • Flush all toilets — check for running or leaks
  • Test all light switches, outlets, and ceiling fans
  • Inspect windows — do they open, close, and lock properly?
  • Check garage door opener (if applicable)
  • Inspect exterior: gutters, downspouts, walkways, porch/deck
  • Landscaping: mow lawn, trim bushes, clear walkways
  • Pest inspection and treatment if needed

📋 Administrative Preparation

  • Lease fully signed by all parties
  • Security deposit collected and deposited in the proper account (per state law)
  • First month's rent collected
  • Renter's insurance policy received and verified (if required in lease)
  • Utility transfer instructions sent to tenant
  • Move-in inspection form prepared (see template below)
  • Welcome packet assembled (see section below)
  • Keys, fobs, garage remotes prepared and labeled
  • Emergency contact information collected from tenant
  • Set up tenant in your property management system

The Move-In Inspection: How to Do It Right

The move-in inspection should be conducted with the tenant present. Walk through every room together, documenting the condition of every surface, fixture, and appliance. Both parties sign the completed form.

When to Do the Inspection

Ideally, the inspection happens on or just before move-in day — after the unit is cleaned and prepped, but before the tenant brings in furniture. Once furniture is in place, you can't inspect walls, floors, or fixtures hidden behind it.

How to Document Condition

For each room and area, note the condition of:

  • Walls: Nail holes, scuffs, stains, cracks, paint condition
  • Floors: Scratches, stains, tears (carpet), chips (tile), warping (wood)
  • Ceiling: Stains, cracks, peeling paint
  • Windows: Cracks, broken seals, screen condition, blinds/coverings
  • Doors: Scratches, dents, hardware function, weather stripping
  • Fixtures: Light fixtures, outlets, switches — all functioning?
  • Appliances: Dents, scratches, functionality
  • Cabinets/Counters: Chips, stains, hardware, drawer function
  • Plumbing: Faucet function, drain speed, toilet operation

Photography Tips for Bulletproof Documentation

Photos are your best friend in a deposit dispute. Here's how to take ones that actually hold up:

  1. Use good lighting. Open blinds, turn on lights. Dark photos hide details — and judges know it.
  2. Take wide shots AND close-ups. Wide shot shows the full room for context. Close-up shows the specific condition of surfaces, stains, or damage.
  3. Photograph every wall in every room. Even if it looks perfect. "No damage documented" is weaker evidence than "photo showing clean wall."
  4. Capture dates. Use a photo app that embeds timestamps, or include a dated paper in the frame.
  5. Don't edit or filter. Raw photos only. Edited photos can be challenged in court.
  6. Take video walkthroughs. A narrated video walkthrough is powerful supplemental evidence. Talk through each room, noting the condition as you go.
  7. Store securely. Cloud backup + local copy. Name files clearly: 2026-03-08_unit4B_kitchen_counter.jpg

📸 Pro tip: Take 50+ photos per unit. Storage is free. Legal disputes are expensive. You can never have too many move-in photos.

Room-by-Room Inspection Template

Use this template as your move-in (and move-out) inspection form. Rate each item as Good, Fair, Poor, or note specific damage.

🏠 Living Room / Common Areas

  • Walls — condition, nail holes, marks
  • Ceiling — stains, cracks
  • Flooring — scratches, stains, damage
  • Windows — glass, locks, screens, blinds
  • Doors — function, locks, condition
  • Light fixtures — working, condition
  • Outlets — working (test with a phone charger)
  • Light switches — all functional
  • Ceiling fan — speed settings, wobble
  • Baseboards — condition, gaps
  • Closets — shelving, rods, doors

🍳 Kitchen

  • Countertops — chips, stains, burns
  • Cabinets — doors, drawers, hinges, interior
  • Sink — faucet, drain, sprayer, disposal
  • Refrigerator — interior, ice maker, seals
  • Stove/Oven — burners, oven function, knobs, drip pans
  • Dishwasher — cycle test, interior, racks
  • Microwave — function, interior, turntable
  • Flooring — under appliances too
  • Backsplash — grout condition, cracks
  • Range hood/exhaust fan — function, filter
  • Pantry — shelving, cleanliness

🛁 Bathrooms

  • Toilet — flush, fill, no running, base caulk
  • Sink — faucet, drain speed, basin condition
  • Tub/Shower — faucet, drain, caulk/grout, showerhead
  • Mirror/Medicine cabinet — condition, hinges
  • Vanity/Countertop — condition, drawers
  • Tile — grout condition, cracks, loose tiles
  • Exhaust fan — function, noise
  • Towel bars/hooks — secure, condition
  • Toilet paper holder — secure
  • Flooring — water damage, stains

🛏️ Bedrooms

  • Walls — condition, all four walls
  • Ceiling — stains, cracks, light fixture
  • Flooring — condition under good lighting
  • Windows — function, locks, screens, blinds
  • Closet — doors/sliders, shelving, rod, light
  • Doors — function, lock (privacy), stops
  • Outlets — working (minimum 2 per room per code)
  • Ceiling fan/light — all functions

🏗️ Exterior / Common Areas

  • Front door — lock, deadbolt, weather stripping, peephole
  • Patio/Balcony — surface, railing, drainage
  • Garage — door function, remote, floor condition
  • Driveway/Parking — assigned spot, condition
  • Mailbox — function, key
  • Storage unit — lock, condition (if applicable)
  • Landscaping — current state (important for yards tenants maintain)

⚡ Systems & Safety

  • HVAC — heating and cooling function, thermostat
  • Water heater — adequate hot water, temperature setting
  • Smoke detectors — one per bedroom + hallway (test all)
  • Carbon monoxide detectors — per code requirements
  • Fire extinguisher — location, charge level
  • Electrical panel — labeled, accessible
  • Water shutoff — location noted for tenant
  • Washer/Dryer — function, hoses, vent (if in-unit)

Utility Transfers

Clear communication about utilities prevents confusion and billing disputes. Here's how to handle it:

Utility Who Pays Transfer Action
Electric Varies by lease Tenant sets up account; landlord cancels theirs on move-in date
Gas Varies by lease Same as electric — coordinate transfer date
Water/Sewer Often landlord If tenant pays, provide account setup info
Trash Often landlord Inform tenant of pickup schedule and rules
Internet/Cable Tenant Provide info on which providers service the property

Pro tip: Send utility transfer instructions at least 7-10 days before move-in. Include provider names, phone numbers, and account setup links. This avoids the "I forgot to set up electric" call on move-in day.

Key and Access Handoff

Document every key, fob, remote, and access code you provide:

  • Door keys: Note how many sets (typically 2 per adult tenant)
  • Mailbox keys: Usually 1-2
  • Garage remotes/codes: Document serial numbers
  • Gate fobs/codes: For gated communities
  • Pool keys: If applicable
  • Smart lock codes: Change from previous tenant's code

Have the tenant sign a key receipt listing every item. This becomes important at move-out when you need keys returned (and can charge for unreturned keys per your lease terms).

🔑 Always rekey locks between tenants. It's inexpensive ($50-$150) and eliminates the risk of previous tenants having access. Many states require it.

The Welcome Packet

A welcome packet is optional but highly recommended. It reduces maintenance calls, prevents lease violations, and makes tenants feel valued. Include:

Essential Information

  • Copy of the signed lease
  • Emergency maintenance contact number (and how to submit non-emergency requests)
  • Your contact information and preferred communication method
  • Rent payment instructions: due date, accepted methods, late fee policy
  • Important property rules (noise, parking, pets, trash, smoking)

Practical Details

  • HVAC filter size and recommended change schedule
  • Trash pickup schedule and recycling instructions
  • Location of: water shutoff, electrical panel, gas shutoff
  • Appliance manuals or quick-reference guides
  • Wi-Fi password (if landlord-provided internet)
  • Local emergency numbers: police non-emergency, fire dept, hospital

Nice Touches That Build Goodwill

  • List of recommended local restaurants, groceries, services
  • Welcome letter with a personal touch
  • Small welcome gift (a gift card, cleaning supplies, or a plant)

These small gestures cost almost nothing but significantly improve tenant satisfaction and retention. Happy tenants stay longer and take better care of your property.

Move-In Day: Step-by-Step Process

Here's the exact sequence for a smooth move-in day:

  1. Arrive 15-30 minutes early. Do a final walkthrough yourself. Make sure everything is clean and working.
  2. Meet the tenant. Greet them professionally. This sets the tone for the entire tenancy.
  3. Walk through the inspection together. Go room by room using your checklist. Note everything — even minor items. Take photos as you go.
  4. Both parties sign the inspection form. Give the tenant a copy immediately (or email it within 24 hours).
  5. Hand over keys. Have them sign the key receipt.
  6. Review the welcome packet. Walk them through the essentials: maintenance requests, rent payment, emergency contacts.
  7. Show them the important stuff. Water shutoff, electrical panel, thermostat, washer/dryer operation, garbage disposal reset button.
  8. Answer questions. Let them ask anything. First-time renters especially will have questions.
  9. Confirm first month's rent and deposit are received. Final accounting check.
  10. Leave them to settle in. Don't overstay. They're excited about their new home — give them space.

After Move-In: Follow-Up

The move-in process doesn't end when you hand over the keys. A quick follow-up builds trust:

  • 48-hour follow-up: Send a quick message asking if everything is working well and if they've found any issues you missed
  • 7-day follow-up: Check in one more time. By now, they've used all appliances and systems and may have spotted problems
  • 30-day check: Optional, but a brief "how's everything going?" shows you care about their experience

Any issues reported in the first few days should be addressed promptly. This is your chance to demonstrate that you're a responsive, professional landlord — which directly impacts tenant retention.

Common Move-In Mistakes to Avoid

1. Skipping the Inspection

Some landlords hand over keys and call it done. Without a documented inspection, you have no baseline for the security deposit. You'll lose every deposit dispute.

2. Rushing Through the Walkthrough

A thorough inspection takes 30-45 minutes. Don't rush it to save time. Those 45 minutes can save you thousands in deposit disputes and maintenance costs.

3. Not Documenting Pre-Existing Damage

If there's a scratch on the hardwood floor or a stain on the carpet, document it NOW. If it's not on the move-in inspection, the tenant can claim it was there when they moved in — and you can't prove otherwise.

4. Forgetting to Rekey Locks

This is a security and liability issue. If a previous tenant (or their ex) has a key and enters the property, you could be liable.

5. Not Transferring Utilities Properly

Gaps in utility service mean the tenant moves into a unit with no electricity or water. Overlap your utility service by one day to prevent this.

6. Having an Unsigned Lease at Move-In

Never hand over keys without a fully signed lease and collected deposits. This seems obvious, but it happens more than you'd think — especially when landlords are eager to fill a vacancy.

Legal Considerations by State

Move-in inspection requirements vary by state. Here's a quick overview:

Requirement States That Require It
Move-in inspection required AZ, GA, MD, MI, VA, WA, WI (among others)
Tenant must be offered the opportunity to inspect Most states
Written checklist required for deposit deductions Many states (check your specific state)
Photos/video explicitly admissible Most jurisdictions accept them

Even if your state doesn't explicitly require a move-in inspection, do one anyway. It's the standard of care in professional property management, and courts will note its absence. Read our Security Deposit Return Guide for details on how this documentation protects you at move-out.
