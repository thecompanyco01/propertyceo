How to Start a Property Management Company in 2026 (Step-by-Step)

March 6, 2026 · 15 min read · By PropertyCEO

So you want to start a property management company. Good — it's one of the most recession-resistant, scalable businesses you can build. But most people who try to start a property management company either never get off the ground or burn out within the first year because they skip critical steps. This guide walks you through every step from idea to first clients, with real costs, timelines, and advice from experienced PM entrepreneurs.

Step 1: Decide If Property Management Is Right for You

Before you start a property management company, let's be honest about what this business actually requires. Property management is a service business — you're dealing with property owners, tenants, vendors, and legal obligations simultaneously. Here's what you need:

  • People skills: You'll handle complaints, negotiate with vendors, and manage owner expectations daily
  • Organization: One missed inspection, lease renewal, or maintenance request can cost you a client
  • Legal knowledge: Fair Housing laws, landlord-tenant law, and trust accounting aren't optional
  • Financial discipline: You're handling other people's money — there's no room for sloppy bookkeeping
  • Patience: It takes 6-12 months to build a sustainable income from property management

💡 The best background for starting a property management company: real estate agent, maintenance professional, real estate investor, or someone who has worked in PM for 2+ years. But none of these are required — they just shorten the learning curve.

Step 2: Research Your Market

Not all markets are equal. Before you start a property management company, research your local market:

Market Research Checklist

  • Rental inventory: How many rental properties exist in your target area? More rentals = more potential clients
  • Competition: How many property managers operate in your market? What do they charge? Read their reviews.
  • Owner demographics: Are there many out-of-state investors? Individual landlords with 1-5 properties? These are your ideal first clients.
  • Rental rates: Higher rents mean higher management fees per door
  • Growth trends: Is your market growing? More construction and investor activity means more future clients

A good starting market has at least 5,000 rental units, fewer than 10 established PM companies, and a growing investor population. Check your local NARPM chapter, Zillow rental data, and Census Bureau data for numbers.

Step 3: Get Licensed

Most states require a real estate broker's license to start a property management company. Some states have specific property management licenses, and a few have minimal requirements. Here's the breakdown:

Licensing Requirements by Category

  • Broker's license required: Most states (CA, TX, FL, NY, etc.) — requires passing a broker exam and meeting experience requirements
  • PM-specific license: Some states offer property management-only licenses (OR, MT, SC)
  • Minimal licensing: A handful of states have limited requirements (VT, ME)

⏱️ Timeline: 2-6 months to get licensed (depending on state and your current status)

For a detailed state-by-state breakdown, see our property management license requirements guide. Don't skip this step — operating without proper licensing can result in fines of $10,000+ and even criminal charges in some states.

Step 4: Set Up Your Business Entity

When you start a property management company, the right legal structure protects your personal assets and sets you up for growth:

Recommended: LLC or S-Corp

  • LLC: Simplest option, provides personal liability protection, pass-through taxation. Cost: $50-$500 to file depending on your state.
  • S-Corp: Better for tax optimization once you're profitable (saves on self-employment tax). Consider converting to S-Corp when you're earning $60,000+ from the business.

Essential Setup Tasks

  1. Register your LLC with the state ($50-$500)
  2. Get an EIN from the IRS (free)
  3. Open a business bank account
  4. Open a trust/escrow account (required in most states for holding owner and tenant funds)
  5. Get a business phone number and professional email
  6. Register your DBA (doing business as) if your company name differs from LLC name

Step 5: Get Insured

Insurance isn't optional when you start a property management company. At minimum, you need:

  • Errors & Omissions (E&O): $1,000-$3,000/year — covers claims of negligence, mistakes, and failure to perform
  • General Liability: $500-$1,500/year — covers bodily injury and property damage claims
  • Cyber Liability: $500-$1,500/year — covers data breaches (you'll have sensitive tenant and owner data)
  • Workers Comp: Required once you have employees (cost varies by state and payroll)

For a complete breakdown, read our property management insurance guide. Budget $2,000-$5,000/year for insurance when starting.

Step 6: Build Your Tech Stack

Your property management software is the backbone of your operations. Here's the minimum tech stack to start a property management company:

Essential Technology

  • Property management software: Buildium ($55-$175/mo), Appfolio ($1.40/unit/mo min $280), or Rent Manager — handles accounting, maintenance, leasing, and communication
  • CRM: Track owner leads and sales pipeline (see our CRM guide)
  • Website: Professional site with lead capture forms ($500-$3,000 to build)
  • Phone system: Google Voice (free) or RingCentral ($20-$35/mo)
  • Accounting: QuickBooks Online ($30-$90/mo) for business accounting (separate from trust accounting)

💰 Monthly tech costs: $150-$500 to start

Step 7: Create Your Management Agreement

Your management agreement is the legal contract between you and property owners. It defines your responsibilities, fees, authority, and liability. Get this wrong and you'll have problems.

Key elements to include:

  • Scope of services (exactly what you will and won't do)
  • Fee structure (management fee, leasing fee, maintenance markup, etc.)
  • Maintenance authorization threshold
  • Term and termination provisions (30-60 day notice is standard)
  • Trust account procedures
  • Owner responsibilities and obligations
  • Insurance requirements
  • Indemnification clauses

For a detailed guide, see our management agreement template guide. Budget $500-$2,000 for an attorney to review or draft your agreement — this is not the place to cut corners.

Step 8: Set Your Pricing

Pricing is where most new PM companies go wrong — they undercharge to win clients, then can't afford to deliver quality service. Here's how to price when you start a property management company:

Recommended Starting Prices

  • Monthly management: 8-10% of collected rent (don't go below 8%)
  • Leasing/placement fee: 50-100% of one month's rent
  • Lease renewal: $200-$300
  • Maintenance markup: 10-15% on vendor invoices
  • Setup fee: $200-$300 per property

🎯 Target: $150-$200 revenue per door per month (all fees combined)

For a deep dive, read our pricing strategy guide and check what property managers charge in your market.

Step 9: Get Your First Clients

This is the hardest part of starting a property management company. Here's how to land your first 10 clients:

Immediate Actions (Week 1-2)

  1. Your personal network: Tell everyone you know you've started a PM company. Friends, family, former colleagues, your real estate contacts. Your first clients almost always come from your network.
  2. Real estate agent partnerships: Call 20 local agents who work with investors. Offer referral fees ($200-$500 per client referred).
  3. Local investor meetups: Attend every REIA meeting, real estate meetup, and investor networking event in your area.
  4. Online presence: Claim your Google Business Profile, create profiles on Yelp and Zillow.

Ongoing Lead Generation (Month 1-3)

  • Google Ads: Bid on "property management [your city]" — expect $10-$30 per click, 5-10% conversion rate
  • Content marketing: Start writing SEO-optimized blog posts targeting local landlord questions
  • Direct mail: Send postcards to out-of-state property owners in your market (public records data)
  • FSBO/FRBO outreach: Contact owners listing properties for rent by owner — they're struggling and need help

For more strategies, see our lead generation guide and owner acquisition scripts.

Step 10: Deliver Exceptional Service

Once you've signed your first clients, your only job is making them glad they hired you. The first 30 days with a new owner set the tone for the entire relationship:

  1. Property onboarding (Days 1-3): Full property inspection, document condition, review lease, meet tenants
  2. Owner communication (Days 1-7): Weekly update emails during the first month — overommunicate early
  3. First maintenance (Days 1-14): Address any outstanding maintenance quickly — show owners you get things done
  4. First owner report (Day 30): Deliver a detailed monthly report with financials, activity summary, and recommendations

Happy owners refer other owners. Your best growth engine is word-of-mouth, and it starts with exceptional service delivery. Read our owner retention guide for the full playbook.

Total Cost to Start a Property Management Company

Startup Budget Breakdown

  • Licensing: $500-$3,000 (varies by state)
  • LLC formation: $50-$500
  • Insurance: $2,000-$5,000/year
  • Attorney (agreements): $500-$2,000
  • Software (first 3 months): $450-$1,500
  • Website: $500-$3,000
  • Marketing (first 3 months): $1,000-$5,000
  • Office (optional — many start from home): $0-$2,000/month

💰 Total: $5,000-$20,000 to start (lean: $5K, comfortable: $15K)

For a more detailed cost breakdown, see our startup cost guide.

Timeline to Profitability

Here's a realistic timeline for starting a property management company:

  • Month 1-2: Setup, licensing, first 2-5 clients from personal network
  • Month 3-4: 10-20 doors, marketing engine starting to produce leads
  • Month 5-6: 25-50 doors, breaking even or slightly profitable
  • Month 7-12: 50-100 doors, consistent profitability, considering first hire
  • Year 2: 100-200 doors, hiring your first employee, building systems

The most successful PM startups reach 50 doors within 6 months and 100 doors within a year. If you're not at 25 doors by month 4, something needs to change — usually your sales and marketing approach.

💡 The fastest way to get to 100 doors: buy an existing PM portfolio. Small PM companies sell for 1-2x annual management fees. A 50-door portfolio might cost $30,000-$60,000 — but you skip 6-12 months of grinding for clients.
