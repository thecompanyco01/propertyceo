How to Start a Property Management Company in 2026

Updated March 2026 · 18 min read

Starting a property management company is one of the most recession-resistant business models in real estate. Landlords always need help managing tenants, collecting rent, and maintaining properties — whether the market is booming or crashing.

The U.S. property management industry generates over $100 billion in annual revenue, and demand continues to grow as more investors buy rental properties but don't want the headaches of managing them. If you're organized, service-oriented, and willing to hustle, this business can generate $100K+ in your first year.

This guide covers everything you need to know to go from zero to your first 50 doors.

Step 1: Understand the Business Model

Property management companies make money primarily through management fees — typically 8-12% of monthly rent collected. Additional revenue comes from:

  • Leasing fees: 50-100% of first month's rent for placing a new tenant
  • Maintenance markups: 10-20% markup on contractor invoices
  • Late fees: Often split between owner and management company
  • Lease renewal fees: $150-$300 per renewal
  • Setup/onboarding fees: $200-$500 per new property

Quick math: Managing 50 properties averaging $1,500/month rent at 10% fee = $7,500/month recurring revenue, plus $3,000-$5,000/month in leasing and ancillary fees. That's $120K-$150K/year from 50 doors.

Step 2: Check Licensing Requirements

Most states require a real estate broker's license to operate a property management company. Some states have specific property management licenses. A few states (like Maine and Vermont) don't require any license at all.

States with specific PM licenses:

  • Montana, Oregon, South Carolina, South Dakota

States that require a broker's license:

  • California, Florida, Texas, New York, Illinois, and most others

States with no PM license required:

  • Maine, Vermont, Massachusetts (limited), Idaho

Pro tip: If you don't have a broker's license, you can start by working under a licensed broker. Many new PM companies form an arrangement where a broker sponsors them in exchange for a small fee or percentage.

Step 4: Handle Legal & Insurance

Business structure

Form an LLC — it protects your personal assets and is the standard structure for PM companies. Cost: $50-$500 depending on your state.

Insurance you need

  • General liability insurance: $500-$1,500/year — covers slip-and-fall, property damage
  • Errors & omissions (E&O) insurance: $1,000-$3,000/year — covers professional mistakes
  • Workers' comp: Required if you have employees
  • Cyber liability: Recommended — you'll handle sensitive tenant data

Trust account

You must set up a separate trust account for holding security deposits and owner funds. Commingling funds with your operating account is illegal in every state and the #1 reason PM companies get shut down.

Step 6: Set Your Pricing

Pricing strategy makes or breaks a new PM company. Price too high and you can't compete. Price too low and you won't survive.

Recommended pricing for new companies:

  • Management fee: 8-10% of rent collected (not rent due — important distinction)
  • Leasing fee: 50-75% of first month's rent
  • Lease renewal: $150-$200
  • Setup fee: $0-$200 (waive this initially to win clients)
  • Maintenance markup: 10% on work over $500

Pricing tip: Don't compete on price alone. Instead, compete on responsiveness and communication. The #1 complaint owners have about property managers is lack of communication. If you respond to every owner call within an hour, you'll win clients even at 10% when competitors charge 8%.

Step 7: Get Your First Clients

This is the hardest part. Here's what actually works:

1. Your personal network

Tell every real estate investor, agent, and landlord you know that you've started a PM company. Ask for referrals. Most successful PM companies get their first 5-10 clients through personal connections.

2. Real estate investor meetups

Attend local REI meetups, REIA chapters, and BiggerPockets events. Don't pitch — build relationships. The deals will come.

3. Real estate agents

Partner with agents who sell investment properties. They constantly have buyers who need management. Offer agents a referral fee ($200-$500 per door) for introductions.

4. Craigslist & Facebook Marketplace

Search for "for rent" listings. These are self-managing landlords. Many of them are overwhelmed and would love to hand off the work. Reach out with a specific value proposition.

5. Google Business Profile

Set up your Google Business Profile immediately. Most property owners Google "property management near me" — you need to show up. Collect reviews from every client.

6. Content marketing & SEO

Start a blog targeting keywords like "property management in [your city]", "landlord tips", and "tenant screening guide". This creates a long-term inbound pipeline.

Step 8: Onboard Properties Professionally

First impressions matter. When you sign a new owner, have a systematic onboarding process:

  1. Execute property management agreement (have a lawyer review your template)
  2. Conduct thorough property inspection with photos
  3. Collect all keys, codes, and vendor contacts
  4. Set up the property in your PM software
  5. Transfer or collect security deposits into trust account
  6. Introduce yourself to existing tenants
  7. Review and audit current lease terms
  8. Set up owner portal access

Step 9: Build Your Vendor Network

Your vendors can make or break your reputation. Build relationships with:

  • General handyman: For small repairs under $500
  • Licensed plumber, electrician, HVAC tech: For specialized work
  • Cleaning crew: For turnover cleans
  • Painter: Touch-ups and full repaints
  • Locksmith: Rekeying between tenants
  • Landscaper: Ongoing yard maintenance

Pro tip: Negotiate volume discounts. "I'll send you all my work if you give me 15% off retail pricing." Vendors love guaranteed volume.

Step 10: Scale to 100+ Doors

Once you have 20-30 doors, you need to systematize everything:

  • Hire a part-time leasing agent at 30-50 doors
  • Hire a maintenance coordinator at 50-75 doors
  • Hire an office manager/bookkeeper at 75-100 doors
  • Document every process — make SOPs for everything from tenant screening to lease renewals
  • Automate everything possible — online rent collection, maintenance requests, lease signing

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Not having a proper management agreement: This is your legal protection. Never manage a property on a handshake.
  • Underpricing your services: You can't provide good service at 5% fees. Price fairly and deliver quality.
  • Neglecting owner communication: Send monthly statements, respond quickly, and proactively report issues. This is how you keep clients.
  • Growing too fast without systems: 50 doors with good processes beats 100 doors in chaos.
  • Commingling funds: Keep trust and operating accounts 100% separate. This is non-negotiable.

The Bottom Line

Starting a property management company requires relatively low capital, generates recurring revenue, and can scale to seven figures. The keys are: get licensed, start lean, obsess over owner communication, and never stop marketing for new doors.

The biggest competitive advantage for a new PM company isn't price — it's responsiveness. If you answer every call, send updates proactively, and treat owners' properties like your own investments, you'll grow faster than you ever expected.
