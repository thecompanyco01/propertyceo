Quitclaim Deed: What It Is, How It Works & When to Use One

March 8, 2026 · 16 min read · By PropertyCEO

A quitclaim deed is one of the most commonly used — and most commonly misunderstood — legal documents in real estate. Whether you're transferring a property into an LLC, removing an ex-spouse from a title after divorce, or gifting real estate to a family member, a quitclaim deed is often the fastest and simplest way to do it.

But that simplicity comes with risks. Unlike a warranty deed, a quitclaim deed offers zero guarantees about the quality of the title being transferred. Use one in the wrong situation and you could end up with a property riddled with liens, encumbrances, or competing ownership claims.

This guide covers everything property managers, investors, and homeowners need to know about quitclaim deeds — when to use them, when to avoid them, how to file one, and the state-specific rules you need to follow.

What Is a Quitclaim Deed?

A quitclaim deed (sometimes incorrectly called a "quit claim deed") is a legal instrument that transfers whatever ownership interest the grantor (the person giving up the property) holds to the grantee (the person receiving it). The key word is whatever.

Here's what makes a quitclaim deed unique: the grantor makes no warranties about what they're transferring. They're essentially saying, "I'm giving you whatever interest I have in this property — which might be full ownership, partial ownership, or nothing at all."

This is fundamentally different from a warranty deed, where the grantor legally guarantees they own the property free and clear and will defend the title against any future claims.

Key point: A quitclaim deed transfers interest, not necessarily ownership. If the grantor has no actual interest in the property, the grantee receives nothing — and has no legal recourse against the grantor.

Quitclaim Deed vs. Warranty Deed: What's the Difference?

This is the most important distinction in deed types, and getting it wrong can cost you thousands of dollars or even a property.

Feature Quitclaim Deed Warranty Deed
Title guarantee None — transfers "as is" Full guarantee of clear title
Protection against liens None Grantor liable for undisclosed liens
Title search required? Not required (but recommended) Always performed
Common use cases Family transfers, divorce, LLC transfers Standard property sales
Title insurance available? Usually not Yes — standard practice
Legal recourse if title defect None against grantor Can sue grantor for damages
Cost to prepare $50–$500 $300–$1,000+

There's also a special warranty deed (sometimes called a limited warranty deed) that sits between these two. It only guarantees the title for the period the grantor owned the property — not before. These are common in bank foreclosure sales and commercial transactions.

When to Use a Quitclaim Deed

Quitclaim deeds are ideal for transfers between parties who already know and trust each other. Here are the most common scenarios:

1. Transferring Property to an LLC

This is the #1 use case for property managers and investors. When you move a rental property from your personal name into an LLC for liability protection, a quitclaim deed is the standard tool. You're transferring to yourself (your entity), so the lack of title warranties doesn't matter — you already know the state of the title.

Warning: Transferring a mortgaged property to an LLC via quitclaim could trigger the due-on-sale clause in your mortgage. Most residential lenders don't enforce this for LLC transfers, but check with your lender first. Consider a DSCR loan that's already in the LLC's name to avoid this issue.

2. Divorce Property Settlements

In a divorce, one spouse often needs to transfer their interest in the marital home to the other spouse. A quitclaim deed handles this cleanly. The court order or divorce decree specifies who gets what, and the quitclaim deed executes the transfer.

3. Family Gifts and Estate Planning

Parents transferring property to children, siblings dividing inherited property, or adding a spouse to the title after marriage — all common quitclaim deed scenarios. Just be aware of potential gift tax implications for transfers without consideration (payment).

4. Clearing Title Defects (Cloud on Title)

If a title search reveals a potential claim from a previous owner, ex-spouse, or heir, a quitclaim deed from that party can clear the title defect. This is common when settling estate disputes or resolving old ownership questions.

5. Adding or Removing a Name from the Title

Adding a new spouse to the title, removing a deceased co-owner, or correcting a misspelled name on the deed can all be done with a quitclaim deed.

When NOT to Use a Quitclaim Deed

Never use a quitclaim deed when buying property from someone you don't know well or trust completely. Specifically:

  • Standard real estate purchases: Always use a warranty deed and get title insurance
  • Buying from strangers: You have zero recourse if the title turns out to be defective
  • Investment property acquisitions: Except when transferring to your own LLC
  • Properties with unclear ownership history: Get a title search and warranty deed
  • Tax sale or foreclosure purchases: Title issues are common — warranty deed is essential

How to File a Quitclaim Deed: Step-by-Step

Filing a quitclaim deed is straightforward, but every detail matters. An error in the legal description or missing notarization can invalidate the entire transfer.

Step 1: Obtain the Correct Form

Get a quitclaim deed form specific to your state. Each state has different requirements for formatting, language, and required elements. You can get forms from your county recorder's office, a real estate attorney, or reputable legal document services.

Step 2: Fill in the Required Information

Every quitclaim deed must include:

  • Grantor's full legal name — exactly as it appears on the current deed
  • Grantee's full legal name — the person or entity receiving the property
  • Legal description of the property — not just the street address; you need the metes and bounds or lot/block description from the current deed or county records
  • Consideration — the amount paid (can be $1 or "love and affection" for gifts)
  • County and state where the property is located
  • Parcel number — some states require this; check your county

Step 3: Sign and Notarize

The grantor must sign the deed in front of a notary public. Some states also require witnesses (Florida requires two witnesses, for example). The grantee typically does not need to sign, but some states require their signature as well.

Step 4: Record with the County

Take the signed, notarized deed to the county recorder's office (or register of deeds) in the county where the property is located. Pay the recording fee (typically $10–$100). Some counties accept electronic recording.

Step 5: Pay Any Transfer Taxes

Many states and counties charge a real estate transfer tax when property changes hands. Rates vary widely — from nothing in some states to over 2% in others. Some transfers are exempt (e.g., divorce transfers, LLC transfers with no change in beneficial ownership).

Pro tip: Always keep a copy of the recorded deed. The county recorder's office will return the original with a recording stamp showing the date, book, and page number. Store this with your other property management documents.

Quitclaim Deed Risks and Pitfalls

The simplicity of a quitclaim deed is a double-edged sword. Here are the risks every property owner should understand:

No Title Protection

The biggest risk is obvious but worth emphasizing: if the grantor doesn't actually own the property, you get nothing. And you can't sue them for it. This is why quitclaim deeds should only be used between trusted parties.

Hidden Liens and Encumbrances

The property could have unpaid tax liens, mechanic's liens, mortgage balances, or judgment liens that the quitclaim deed doesn't address. These obligations transfer with the property, not with the person.

Due-on-Sale Clause Trigger

If the property has a mortgage, transferring ownership via quitclaim could technically trigger the due-on-sale clause, allowing the lender to demand immediate full payment. While rare in practice for transfers between family or to an LLC, it's a real risk you need to evaluate. Compare this with subject-to financing strategies that also face this risk.

Tax Implications

Transferring property can trigger gift taxes (if no consideration is paid), capital gains taxes, or affect your property tax assessment. Some states reassess property value upon transfer, even between family members. Consult a tax professional before any transfer.

Medicaid Look-Back Period

If you transfer property via quitclaim deed to protect it from Medicaid estate recovery, be aware that Medicaid has a 5-year look-back period. Transfers made within this window can result in a penalty period during which Medicaid benefits are denied.

State-Specific Considerations

Quitclaim deed requirements vary significantly by state. Here are key differences to be aware of:

State Key Requirements
California Requires notarization, documentary transfer tax ($1.10 per $1,000), Preliminary Change of Ownership Report (PCOR)
Florida Requires two witnesses plus notarization, documentary stamp tax ($0.70 per $100)
Texas No state transfer tax, but quitclaim deeds are disfavored — "deeds without warranty" are more common
New York Called "bargain and sale deed without covenants," requires TP-584 tax form, transfer tax of $2 per $500
Illinois Requires notarization, transfer declaration form, state and county transfer taxes
Ohio Requires conveyance fee statement, transfer tax ($1 per $1,000 at county level)

Some states — like North Carolina — have specific statutory forms for quitclaim deeds. Others are more flexible. Always check your state's requirements or consult a local real estate attorney.

Quitclaim Deeds for Property Managers

If you're a property manager or real estate investor, quitclaim deeds come up regularly in your operations:

LLC Transfers for Asset Protection

Moving properties into LLCs is one of the most effective asset protection strategies for landlords. Each rental property ideally sits in its own LLC (or a series LLC where available), shielding your personal assets from lawsuits. The quitclaim deed is the transfer mechanism.

Here's the typical process:

  1. Form the LLC (single-member for simplicity)
  2. Prepare a quitclaim deed from yourself to the LLC
  3. Sign, notarize, and record the deed
  4. Update your landlord insurance to reflect the new owner entity
  5. Notify your lender (if applicable) — some require this
  6. Update the lease to reflect the new landlord entity

Partnership Dissolutions

When real estate partnerships dissolve, quitclaim deeds are used to transfer individual properties to the partners who are taking ownership. This is cleaner than selling and rebuying, and can often be structured tax-efficiently.

Estate Transfers

Inherited properties often need to go through multiple title transfers — from the decedent to the estate, from the estate to heirs, and potentially from heirs to an LLC. Quitclaim deeds facilitate many of these transfers.

Quitclaim Deed Template: What to Include

While you should always use a state-specific form, here are the essential elements every quitclaim deed must contain:

  1. Title: "Quitclaim Deed" clearly stated at the top
  2. Date: The date of execution
  3. Grantor information: Full legal name and address
  4. Grantee information: Full legal name and address
  5. Consideration statement: "For and in consideration of [amount]..."
  6. Granting clause: "...does hereby remise, release, and quitclaim unto the grantee..."
  7. Legal description: Full property description from the existing deed
  8. Grantor's signature and date
  9. Notary acknowledgment block
  10. Witness signatures (if required by state)
  11. Prepared by statement: Name and address of the person who prepared the deed
  12. Return address: Where the recorded deed should be mailed

Do not use generic online templates without verifying they meet your state's specific requirements. A deed that doesn't comply with state law may be rejected by the county recorder or, worse, may not legally transfer the property.

Tax Implications of Quitclaim Deeds

Gift Tax

If you transfer property via quitclaim deed without receiving fair market value in return, the IRS may consider it a gift. As of 2026, the annual gift tax exclusion is $18,000 per recipient. Gifts above this amount must be reported on Form 709, though they may not result in actual tax if they fall within the lifetime exclusion ($12.92 million in 2026).

Capital Gains Tax

The grantee's tax basis in the property depends on the type of transfer. For gifts, the grantee receives the grantor's original cost basis (carryover basis). For inherited property, the basis is stepped up to fair market value at the date of death. This can make a huge difference in capital gains when the property is eventually sold.

Property Tax Reassessment

Some states (notably California under Proposition 13) reassess property taxes when ownership changes. However, many states exempt transfers between spouses, parent-child transfers, and transfers to trusts or LLCs where beneficial ownership doesn't change. Check your state's rules carefully.

Frequently Asked Questions About Quitclaim Deeds

What is a quitclaim deed?

A quitclaim deed is a legal document that transfers whatever ownership interest the grantor has in a property to another party (the grantee), without making any warranties or guarantees about the title. Unlike a warranty deed, it does not promise that the title is free of liens or encumbrances.

Is a quitclaim deed legally binding?

Yes, a quitclaim deed is legally binding once it is properly signed, notarized, and recorded with the county recorder's office. However, it only transfers whatever interest the grantor actually holds — if they have no interest, nothing transfers.

Can a quitclaim deed be reversed?

A quitclaim deed generally cannot be reversed once it is signed, notarized, and recorded. To undo the transfer, the grantee would need to execute a new quitclaim deed transferring the property back. In cases of fraud, duress, or lack of mental capacity, a court may void the deed.

What is the difference between a quitclaim deed and a warranty deed?

A warranty deed guarantees that the grantor holds clear title and will defend against any claims, while a quitclaim deed makes no such guarantees. Warranty deeds are used in standard real estate sales; quitclaim deeds are used between trusted parties like family members, divorcing spouses, or for LLC transfers.

Does a quitclaim deed affect the mortgage?

A quitclaim deed transfers ownership but does NOT remove the grantor from the mortgage. The original borrower remains responsible for mortgage payments unless the lender agrees to a loan assumption or the mortgage is refinanced. Transferring via quitclaim could also trigger a due-on-sale clause.

Do I need a lawyer for a quitclaim deed?

While not always legally required, consulting a real estate attorney is strongly recommended. A lawyer can ensure the deed is properly drafted, identify potential tax implications, verify the legal description is accurate, and confirm the transfer complies with state-specific requirements.

How much does it cost to file a quitclaim deed?

Filing costs typically range from $10 to $100 for recording fees, depending on your county. If you use a lawyer, expect to pay $150–$500 for preparation. Some states also charge transfer taxes, which can be a percentage of the property value. Total costs usually range from $50 to $1,000+.

Bottom Line

A quitclaim deed is a powerful tool for simple property transfers between trusted parties — family members, spouses, business partners, or your own LLC. It's fast, inexpensive, and gets the job done without the overhead of a full title search and warranty deed.

But it is not a substitute for a warranty deed in any transaction involving strangers, significant money, or uncertain title history. The lack of title guarantees means you're taking on all the risk as the grantee.

For property managers and investors, quitclaim deeds are an essential part of the toolkit — especially for LLC structuring and asset protection. Just make sure you understand the tax implications, check your state's requirements, and consult with a real estate attorney when in doubt.
